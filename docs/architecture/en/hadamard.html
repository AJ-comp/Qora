Understanding the H Gate with Arrows

A clear, step-by-step explanation of qubit states, amplitudes, and interference

This page explains the most fundamental quantum gate, the H gate (Hadamard gate), using "arrows that represent amplitudes" instead of complicated matrix math. In particular, we check why applying the H gate twice to the |0⟩ state brings it back to the |0⟩ state, by adding up the amplitudes of the possible measurement outcomes 0 and 1 one step at a time.

1A qubit state has an amplitude for each possible measurement outcome

When you measure a single qubit, the measurement outcome is either 0 or 1. Before measurement, the qubit state carries an amplitude for possible measurement outcome 0 and an amplitude for possible measurement outcome 1. In this page we draw the amplitude of each measurement outcome as an arrow. The length of the arrow is the magnitude of the amplitude, and the direction of the arrow (right/left) is the sign of the amplitude (+/−).

Let's start with the simplest qubit state, the |0⟩ state. In the |0⟩ state, the amplitude of measurement outcome 0 is 1, and the amplitude of measurement outcome 1 is 0.

Possible measurement outcomeAmplitude arrowAmplitude value
0───▶1 (full)
1·0 (none)

The qubit state whose amplitude for measurement outcome 0 is 1 and whose amplitude for measurement outcome 1 is 0 is called |0⟩.

The square of the amplitude length attached to each measurement outcome = the probability of getting that measurement outcome

So if you measure the |0⟩ state, the probability of getting measurement outcome 0 is 1² = 100%, and the probability of getting measurement outcome 1 is 0² = 0%. In other words, the measurement outcome is always 0. The direction and sign of the amplitude are only used from Section 2 onward, so in Section 1 you only need to look at the amplitude's length.

2After one H gate, a single qubit has amplitudes for both measurement outcomes

The H gate does not leave the amplitude gathered at measurement outcome 0 sitting only at measurement outcome 0. The H gate splits the amplitude gathered at measurement outcome 0 into an amplitude contribution going to measurement outcome 0 and an amplitude contribution going to measurement outcome 1. When you apply the H gate to the |0⟩ state, the amplitude 1 that was at measurement outcome 0 turns into an amplitude of 1/√2 for measurement outcome 0 and an amplitude of 1/√2 for measurement outcome 1.

It does not become the |01⟩ state The result of applying the H gate to the |0⟩ state is a state in which one single qubit simultaneously has amplitudes for measurement outcome 0 and measurement outcome 1. Written as a formula, it is (|0⟩ + |1⟩) / √2. |01⟩ is a state notation used when there are two qubits.
Possible measurement outcomeAmplitude before the H gateAmplitude after the H gateMeasurement probability after the H gate
0───▶ 1──▶ 1/√2 ≈ 0.70750%
1· 0──▶ 1/√2 ≈ 0.70750%

After the H gate is applied, the amplitude arrow of measurement outcome 0 gets shorter. The amplitude's length shrinks from 1 to 1/√2 (about 0.707). At the same time, the amplitude of measurement outcome 1, which was 0 before the H gate, newly appears as 1/√2. So if you measure the qubit after applying the H gate, the probability of getting measurement outcome 0 is 50%, and the probability of getting measurement outcome 1 is also 50%.

3Why is the amplitude 1/√2? — Squaring an amplitude gives a probability

If measurement outcomes 0 and 1 each come up with 50% probability, it might look like the amplitudes should each be 1/2. But the amplitude after the H gate is 1/√2, not 1/2. The reason is that the rule probability = amplitude² and the rule that the probabilities of all measurement outcomes add up to 1 must hold at the same time.

If you set both the amplitude of measurement outcome 0 and the amplitude of measurement outcome 1 to 1:

1² + 1² = 2 = 200%   ❌ (a probability cannot be 200%)

So we divide both the amplitude of measurement outcome 0 and the amplitude of measurement outcome 1 by √2 so that the total probability becomes 100%:

(1/√2)² + (1/√2)² = ½ + ½ = 1 = 100%   ✓
Amplitude ↔ probability (one rule, two directions) If probability = amplitude², then conversely amplitude = √probability. (The same single equation as area = side² ↔ side = √area.) So a quantum state records amplitudes — the "square roots of probabilities" — and when you measure a qubit, you square the amplitudes to compute probabilities.

In a picture — the amplitude is the "side" of a square, the probability is its "area"

If you cut the area (probability) in half (100% → 50%), the side (amplitude) shrinks not to half (0.5) but to 1/√2 (≈0.707). That is because the area of a square is the square of its side. So the amplitude after the H gate is 1/√2, not 1/2.

This is not an analogy — it is an actual law of physics In waves (light, sound), the intensity (the brightness of light, the loudness of sound) is proportional to the square of the amplitude.
So to cut the brightness of light in half, you must lower the amplitude not by a factor of 0.5 but by 0.707 (1/√2). This 1/√2 is the same number as the 1/√2 that appears in the H gate.

Probability in quantum mechanics uses the same squaring rule — |amplitude|² = probability; this squaring rule is the Born rule.
That is why quantum mechanics also uses the word "amplitude".

(What gets squared is the amplitude. Frequency is a different axis — the color of light, the pitch of sound — and does not enter the probability.)

4The exact amplitude transformation rule of the H gate

We can first look at how the H gate changes amplitudes as one big rule, and then break the calculation into four steps.

① Split each input amplitude into amplitude contributions toward measurement outcome 0 and measurement outcome 1  →  ② Multiply each amplitude contribution by a sign and 1/√2, then add the contributions arriving at the same measurement outcome

Let's see what happens when we apply the H gate to the |0⟩ state — the state whose amplitude for measurement outcome 0 is 1 and whose amplitude for measurement outcome 1 is 0.

The amplitude arrow of measurement outcome 0 gets shorter, from 1 to 1/√2, and measurement outcome 1, which had no amplitude, gains a new amplitude of 1/√2. This decrease of the amplitude at measurement outcome 0 and creation of the amplitude at measurement outcome 1 is the change caused by applying the H gate to the |0⟩ state.

Which input amplitude contributes to which measurement outcome's amplitude?

Step 1

Name the two amplitudes before the H gate

The H gate does not use just one of the input state's amplitudes. It uses both the amplitude of measurement outcome 0 and the amplitude of measurement outcome 1 before the gate.

SymbolMeaningValue in the |0⟩ state
a₀ Amplitude of measurement outcome 0 before the H gate 1
a₁ Amplitude of measurement outcome 1 before the H gate 0
Step 2

Each input amplitude sends amplitude contributions to both measurement outcomes

The amplitude of measurement outcome 0 before the gate sends contributions to measurement outcomes 0 and 1, and the amplitude of measurement outcome 1 before the gate also sends contributions to measurement outcomes 0 and 1. So four amplitude contributions arise in total.

Departing input amplitude Contribution going to measurement outcome 0 Contribution going to measurement outcome 1
Amplitude of measurement outcome 0 before the gate (a₀) + a₀ × 1/√2 + a₀ × 1/√2
Amplitude of measurement outcome 1 before the gate (a₁) + a₁ × 1/√2 − a₁ × 1/√2
Step 3

Add the amplitude contributions that arrive at the same measurement outcome

Adding the two amplitude contributions that arrive at measurement outcome 0 gives the amplitude of measurement outcome 0 after the H gate. Adding the two amplitude contributions that arrive at measurement outcome 1 gives the amplitude of measurement outcome 1 after the H gate.

Amplitude after the H gateAmplitude contributions to addGeneral formula
Amplitude of measurement outcome 0 contribution from 0 + contribution from 1 a₀ × 1/√2 + a₁ × 1/√2
Amplitude of measurement outcome 1 contribution from 0 + contribution from 1 a₀ × 1/√2 + a₁ × (−1/√2)
Step 4

Substitute the actual amplitude values of the |0⟩ state

In the |0⟩ state, a₀ = 1 and a₁ = 0. So we just put the two amplitude values named in Step 1 into the general formulas of Step 3.

What we computeSubstituted formulaResult
Amplitude of measurement outcome 0 after the H gate 1 × 1/√2 + 0 × 1/√2 1/√2
Amplitude of measurement outcome 1 after the H gate 1 × 1/√2 + 0 × (−1/√2) 1/√2

The sign rule — of the four amplitude-transfer combinations, only one gets a minus

In the H gate's transformation formula, when computing the amplitude of measurement outcome 1 after the gate, only the amplitude of measurement outcome 1 before the gate is multiplied by −1/√2. Where the minus sign goes is determined by which measurement outcome the amplitude comes from and which measurement outcome it goes to.

Key sign rule Of the four amplitude-transfer combinations, only the combination coming from measurement outcome 1 and going to measurement outcome 1 gets a minus sign. Written briefly, this combination is the 1 → 1 term. It means "the amplitude is on the 1 side before the gate, and it contributes to the 1 side after the gate."
Amplitude before the H gateAmplitude contribution going to measurement outcome 0Amplitude contribution going to measurement outcome 1
Amplitude of measurement outcome 0+ ▶ 1/√2+ ▶ 1/√2
Amplitude of measurement outcome 1+ ▶ 1/√2− ◀ 1/√2

The amplitude contribution going to measurement outcome 0 is always positive. The amplitude contribution going to measurement outcome 1 is positive if the amplitude came from measurement outcome 0, and negative if the amplitude came from measurement outcome 1.

5Why the term coming from measurement outcome 1 and going to measurement outcome 1 gets a minus sign

Step 1

A quantum gate must not lose input information

The H gate is a quantum gate, so it must be reversible. Being reversible means that different input states must not merge into the same output state.

Step 2

Without the minus sign, two input states merge into the same output state

If you strip the minus sign out of the H gate, the |0⟩ state and the |1⟩ state go to the same output state.

Without the minus sign

Input |0⟩ (|0⟩ + |1⟩) / √2
Input |1⟩ (|0⟩ + |1⟩) / √2

The two input states become the same output state. Looking only at the output, you cannot tell what the original input was.

With the minus sign

Input |0⟩ (|0⟩ + |1⟩) / √2 = |+⟩
Input |1⟩ (|0⟩ − |1⟩) / √2 = |−⟩

The two output states are orthogonal to each other. They remain two completely distinguishable states.

Step 3

The minus sign is needed to make the output states distinguishable

The result of applying the H gate to the |0⟩ state and the result of applying the H gate to the |1⟩ state must be not just slightly different but two completely distinguishable directions (orthogonal).

What creates the orthogonality of the two output states is the minus sign of the term coming from measurement outcome 1 and going to measurement outcome 1.

Step 4

In the current notation, the minus sign sits on the term coming from measurement outcome 1 and going to measurement outcome 1

We write the result of applying the H gate to the |0⟩ state as (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2, and adopt the notational convention that the amplitude contribution going to measurement outcome 0 stays positive.

Under this convention, for the result of applying the H gate to the |1⟩ state to be orthogonal to H gate(|0⟩), the term on the measurement outcome 1 side must carry the opposite sign.

Key point The minus sign is not an arbitrarily inserted symbol — it is the sign the H gate needs in order to be reversible. In the current notational convention, that sign sits on the term coming from measurement outcome 1 and going to measurement outcome 1. Thanks to this sign, applying the H gate twice returns you to the initial state.

6Applying the H gate twice revives the |0⟩ state through interference

If you apply the H gate to the |0⟩ state twice in a row, the qubit state returns to the |0⟩ state. For a classical 50/50 coin flip, you might think "going through the randomizing process twice still gives 50/50." But the H gate is not a coin flip — it is a quantum gate that adds amplitudes. Let's add the amplitudes ourselves to see why the state becomes |0⟩ again.

The key to the return Just apply the H gate amplitude transformation formula of Section 4 once more, as is, to the state after the first H gate. Substituting, the amplitude of measurement outcome 0 is reinforced to +1, and the amplitude of measurement outcome 1 is canceled to 0.
Step 1

Take the amplitudes after the first H gate as the input of the second H gate

In Section 4 we named the input amplitudes of the H gate transformation formula a₀ and a₁. When applying the second H gate, the amplitudes produced by the first H gate are exactly a₀ and a₁.

SymbolMeaning before the second H gateValue
a₀ Amplitude of measurement outcome 0 after the first H gate 1/√2
a₁ Amplitude of measurement outcome 1 after the first H gate 1/√2
Step 2

Use the H gate amplitude transformation formula of Section 4 once more, as is

There is no extra rule to memorize. Just substitute a₀ = 1/√2 and a₁ = 1/√2 into the general formulas built in Section 4.

Amplitude after the second H gateGeneral formula from Section 4Substituted formula
Amplitude of measurement outcome 0 a₀ × 1/√2 + a₁ × 1/√2 (1/√2) × 1/√2 + (1/√2) × 1/√2
Amplitude of measurement outcome 1 a₀ × 1/√2 + a₁ × (−1/√2) (1/√2) × 1/√2 + (1/√2) × (−1/√2)
Step 3

Evaluating the substituted formulas makes the return immediately visible

Since (1/√2) × (1/√2) = 1/2, the two amplitudes after the second H gate are computed as follows.

Amplitude contributions arriving at measurement outcome 0
+1/2 + +1/2 = +1
Same sign, so constructive interference occurs.

The amplitude of measurement outcome 0 survives. The measurement probability is 100%.

Amplitude contributions arriving at measurement outcome 1
+1/2 + −1/2 = 0
Opposite signs, so destructive interference occurs.

The amplitude of measurement outcome 1 vanishes. The measurement probability is 0%.

H gate(H gate(|0⟩)) = |0⟩ Result of the second H gate Amplitude of measurement outcome 0 = +1 (measurement probability 100%) Amplitude of measurement outcome 1 = 0 (measurement probability 0%) So we are back in the |0⟩ state
Why the measurement after the second H gate is fixed at 0 instead of 50/50

Two amplitude contributions arrive at measurement outcome 1. The amplitude contribution coming from the |0⟩ component is +1/2, and the amplitude contribution coming from the |1⟩ component is −1/2.

The two amplitude contributions arriving at measurement outcome 1 have opposite signs, so they add up to 0. This vanishing of the amplitude of measurement outcome 1 is destructive interference.

At the same time, the two amplitude contributions arriving at measurement outcome 0 are +1/2 and +1/2, so they add up to +1.

The −1/2 on the measurement outcome 1 side comes exactly from the rule in Section 4: "the term coming from measurement outcome 1 and going to measurement outcome 1 gets a −."

What if there were no minus?

The amplitude of measurement outcome 1 would become +1/2 + 1/2 = +1 and would not have canceled.

At the same time, the amplitude of measurement outcome 0 would also become +1, so adding the probabilities of the two measurement outcomes would exceed 1 — an invalid transformation.

The minus sign of the term coming from measurement outcome 1 and going to measurement outcome 1 cancels the amplitude of measurement outcome 1 and keeps the total probability at 1.

Try it in Qora

Write the circuit that applies the H gate twice in Qora

The Qora code below prepares one qubit, then applies the H gate twice to that same qubit and measures it. It is the H gate(H gate(|0⟩)) = |0⟩ circuit computed in this page.

Qora code
operation Main() {
    // Prepare one qubit.
    // Right after preparation, q[0] is in the |0⟩ state.
    // Amplitude of measurement outcome 0 = 1, amplitude of measurement outcome 1 = 0.
    use q = Qubit[1];

    // Apply the first H gate to q[0].
    // The |0⟩ state turns into the (|0⟩ + |1⟩) / √2 state.
    // Amplitude of measurement outcome 0 = 1/√2.
    // Amplitude of measurement outcome 1 = 1/√2.
    H(q[0]);

    // Apply the second H gate to the same q[0] again.
    // Amplitude of measurement outcome 0 = +1/2 + +1/2 = +1.
    // Amplitude of measurement outcome 1 = +1/2 + -1/2 = 0.
    H(q[0]);

    // Measure q[0] after the second H gate.
    // Probability of measurement outcome 0 = 100%.
    // Probability of measurement outcome 1 = 0%.
    bit result = M(q[0]);
}

The thing to check is that H(q[0]); appears twice in a row. In an ideal execution environment, measurement outcome 0 comes up 100% of the time after the second H gate. The // comments in the Qora code do not affect execution.

7Are amplitudes and interference real physics, or just a notational convention?

The arrow calculations in this page are not a notation game where we simply say "let's define it this way." They put into formulas the reinforcement and cancellation of amplitudes actually observed in nature. Amplitudes and interference are not arbitrarily invented devices but physical concepts needed to explain experimental results.

Notational convention

The parts humans chose

The name "H gate", the way of drawing positive and negative amplitudes as right/left arrows, and notations like |0⟩ and |1⟩ are conventions chosen by humans.

Real physics

The parts confirmed by experiment

The phenomenon of amplitude contributions reinforcing or canceling each other, and the phenomenon that applying the H gate twice brings the |0⟩ state back to the |0⟩ state, are not notational conventions but physical content that explains experimental results.

Which phenomena confirm this?

Evidence 1

Double-slit interference pattern

Even when electrons or photons are sent through two slits one at a time, bright and dark bands build up on the screen.

The dark bands appear at the positions where the amplitude contributions arriving at that spot on the screen cancel each other out.

constructive interference → bright band
destructive interference → dark band
Evidence 2

The double-H-gate circuit

In an ideal quantum circuit or a quantum simulator, applying the H gate twice to the |0⟩ state and measuring gives measurement outcome 0 100% of the time.

Even on real quantum computing hardware, noise only blurs the result slightly — measurement outcome 0 comes up overwhelmingly more often than measurement outcome 1.

|0⟩ → H gate → (|0⟩ + |1⟩) / √2 → H gate → |0⟩
Why we can say it is "real"

If a qubit were a simple 50/50 coin, applying the H gate twice would be the same as "going through a randomizing process twice," and the final measurement probabilities would still be 50/50.

But the ideal double-H-gate circuit returns to the |0⟩ state. Explaining this result requires putting into the calculation something a coin does not have: "signed amplitude contributions canceling each other out."

So the sign of an amplitude and interference are not mere wordplay — they are physical quantities needed to explain experiments.

If you keep asking the deeper question of "why does nature follow these quantum rules," you eventually reach the basic postulates of the current theory, but the fact that the H gate's amplitude transformation rule explains and predicts experimental results has been confirmed again and again.

8One-page summary

State

A qubit state is expressed with amplitudes, not with probabilities themselves

A single-qubit state is expressed by the amplitude attached to each of the possible measurement outcomes 0 and 1.

measurement probability = amplitude length²
Normalization

The probabilities must add up to exactly 1

If the two measurement outcomes have probabilities 50% and 50%, each measurement outcome's amplitude is 1/√2, not 1/2.

(1/√2)² + (1/√2)² = 1
H gate

The H gate splits each input amplitude toward both measurement outcomes

Each input amplitude becomes amplitude contributions toward measurement outcome 0 and measurement outcome 1. Amplitude contributions arriving at the same measurement outcome are added together.

Only the term coming from measurement outcome 1 and going to measurement outcome 1 gets a −.
H gate twice

Applying the same transformation formula once more returns the original state

After the first H gate, a₀ = 1/√2 and a₁ = 1/√2. Just put these values back into the H gate transformation formula.

measurement outcome 0 side: +1/2 + +1/2 = +1
measurement outcome 1 side: +1/2 + −1/2 = 0
Real physics

Amplitudes and interference are not computational decoration but physical concepts that explain experimental results

The bright and dark bands of the double slit, and the circuit result that applying the H gate twice returns to the |0⟩ state, can only be explained by including the constructive and destructive interference of amplitudes.

The core idea of quantum algorithms = designing the destructive and constructive interference of amplitudes —
the process of making the amplitudes of unwanted measurement outcomes cancel, and the amplitudes of wanted measurement outcomes reinforce.
